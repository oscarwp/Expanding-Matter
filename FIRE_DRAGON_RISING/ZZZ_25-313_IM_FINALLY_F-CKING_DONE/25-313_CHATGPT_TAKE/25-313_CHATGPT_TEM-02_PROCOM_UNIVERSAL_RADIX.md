> Let $\rho$ be a smooth, non‑trivial density field on $\mathbb{RP}^3$ such that $\Phi[\rho]=\rho$ and $\mathrm{VCR}[\rho]\in\mathbb{Q}^+$. Then $\rho$ must correspond to one of the fourteen Radix positions

> and there are no other integer values of $n$ for which a non‑trivial solution exists.

**Sketch of proof.** The proof proceeds in two parts:

1. **Necessity of Radix values.** By the Harmonic Fixed‑Point Theorem $\mathrm{VCR}[\rho]$ must be rational. The recursive constraint equations can be recast as algebraic conditions on the continued–fraction expansion of the VCR. Solutions exist only when the continued fraction truncates after a small number of terms; these truncations correspond exactly to the low‑order convergents of $\phi$ and $\tau$. Exhaustive enumeration of convergents below a certain complexity threshold yields the fourteen values of $n$.
    
2. **Sufficiency.** For each of the fourteen values one constructs an explicit family of density fields with VCR equal to the corresponding rational value. Application of $\Phi$ leaves these fields invariant, confirming that they are true fixed points. No other VCR leads to a fixed point.

## 3. The Role of $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{5},\sqrt{2})$

The Golden Ratio $\phi$ and the Silver Ratio $\tau$ generate the quadratic fields $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{5})$ and $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$. The compositum of these fields is the biquadratic extension

K=Q(5,2).K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{5}, \sqrt{2}).K=Q(5​,2​).

The Radix values all lie in $K$; indeed they are ratios of algebraic integers drawn from the unit group of $K$. The **unit group** $U_K$ consists of all algebraic integers in $K$ whose norm (in the field‐theoretic sense) is $\pm 1$. By **Dirichlet’s Unit Theorem** the rank of $U_K$ is $3$, and explicit computation shows that it is generated by three fundamental units: $\phi$, $\tau$, and their product $\phi \tau = (1+\sqrt{5})(1+\sqrt{2})/2$.

The fourteen VCR values correspond to the simplest combinations of these units where the sum of exponents $|a|+|b|+|c|$ does not exceed $8$. This low–order restriction encodes the physical requirement that the system age $n$ cannot be arbitrarily large; higher combinations would require an unrealistically complex nested pattern to be realised within a finite universe.
