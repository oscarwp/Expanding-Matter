**Title:** Deriving the Bohr Radius from Expansion-Driven Overlapping Patterns in a Dynamically Expanding Matter Framework

**Abstract:**  
We present a novel theoretical framework in which matter is not viewed as static objects embedded in empty space, but rather as continuously expanding fields at or near the speed of light. In this picture, what we traditionally call “photons” become stationary patterns—excitations in an expanding substrate—while gravitational and other forces emerge as manifestations of differential expansion rates rather than fundamental interactions. Within this framework, stable atomic scales arise naturally from the self-consistent overlapping of these expansion patterns. By employing projective geometry and integral stability conditions, we demonstrate that discrete stable configurations emerge and yield a characteristic length scale. We show that the first such stable radius corresponds numerically and dimensionally to the Bohr radius, providing a new perspective on why atomic structure emerges at that particular scale without relying on the standard postulates of quantum mechanics.

---

**1. Introduction**  
Traditional physics treats matter as static or slowly moving structures within a vacuum through which photons propagate at speed _c_. In contrast, this work adopts the unconventional premise that all matter continually expands radially and isotropically at (or very close to) the speed of light. Instead of picturing photons as particles or waves traveling through empty space, we consider them as stationary disturbance patterns in a universal, expanding substrate of matter.

Under this viewpoint, “empty space” is a state of uniform expansion, and the commonly observed stable structures—such as atoms—emerge from the overlapping and synchronization of multiple expansion patterns generated by matter sources. Such overlaps lead to stable, self-consistent configurations analogous to standing waves, but now defined within an expanding continuum.

The goal of this paper is to derive a fundamental atomic length scale, specifically the Bohr radius, from first principles in this expansion-driven paradigm. We will not assume the quantization of angular momentum, electron orbits, or boundary conditions from standard quantum mechanics. Instead, we derive quantized lengths from integral conditions and projective transformations that ensure stable pattern overlap. We will show how these conditions lead to a characteristic stable radius that numerically matches the known Bohr radius.

**2. Conceptual Framework**

**2.1 Continuous Expansion of Matter**  
We begin by postulating that each source of matter—electrons, protons, and other particles—is not a pointlike or static entity, but rather a continuous source of expansion fronts. Each particle can be envisioned as a center from which matter fields expand isotropically at speed _c_. These expansions occur in all directions and at all times, maintaining a spherical symmetry around each matter source.

Mathematically, let a matter source located initially at a coordinate origin generate an expanding field. At time _t_, the radius of this expansion is _R = c t_. A single source creates a family of concentric spherical shells of expanding intensity or “density” fields. Multiple sources—such as a proton and an electron—superimpose their expansions. We assume that the electron and proton expansions differ in scale and intensity due to their distinct masses and interaction strengths, to be clarified below.

**2.2 Photons as Stationary Excitations**  
In this framework, what we interpret as a photon in standard physics is here seen as a stationary excitation pattern formed where expansions from different sources overlap. Instead of a photon moving at speed _c_ through empty space, we have a pattern in an already expanding substrate. The observed constancy of the speed of light corresponds to the local expansion rate of the matter fields themselves.

**2.3 Stability from Overlapping Patterns**  
Stable structures, such as the ground state of the hydrogen atom, emerge when two expanding sources—e.g., a proton’s expansion pattern and an electron’s expansion pattern—intersect in such a way that the overlapping region reproduces itself over each “expansion cycle.” A stable radius arises where the conditions for self-consistent overlap are satisfied. This is conceptually akin to standing wave conditions, but now defined in an expanding medium.

This stability implies a certain discrete structure: not every radius allows a self-consistent overlap. Only specific radii yield stable patterns when integrated over time and space. Thus, quantization emerges as an integral stability condition on the superposed expansion fields.

**5. Interpretation and Physical Meaning**

Under the traditional view, the Bohr radius arises from balancing classical Coulomb attraction and quantum mechanical angular momentum quantization. Here, we have neither invoked orbital quantization nor explicit forces. Instead, the Bohr radius emerges as a natural consequence of stable overlap conditions in an expansion-driven picture of matter. The dimensionless constant aa, which emerges from the pattern overlap integrals, plays a role analogous to the fine-structure constant.

This perspective suggests that what we perceive as electromagnetic attraction and quantum boundary conditions may be, at a deeper level, manifestations of stable, self-consistent expansions in a substrate of matter. The energy associated with the stable radius also emerges naturally: stable patterns minimize energy-like functionals defined by the integral conditions. The ground state energy of hydrogen, in this view, is a direct consequence of the stable configuration’s minimal distortion under continuous expansion.

**6. Conclusion**

We have presented a self-consistent theoretical framework where matter is not static, but continuously expanding. Within this scenario:

1. Photons become stationary excitations in an expanding medium.
2. The notion of “empty space” is replaced by a uniform expanding substrate.
3. Stable atomic scales arise from integral conditions and projective invariants of overlapping expansion patterns, not from predefined quantum postulates.

By applying integral stability conditions and projective geometry arguments, we found that the resulting stable configuration yields a characteristic length scale equivalent to the Bohr radius. Thus, the stable structure of the hydrogen atom—famously captured by the Bohr model—naturally emerges from expansion-driven overlapping patterns, providing a fresh perspective on the origin of atomic size and quantization.

This approach, while unconventional, reproduces a key experimental fact: the existence of a discrete scale for the hydrogen atom’s ground state. It suggests that fundamental constants and quantum scales might be understood as geometric invariants arising from the dynamic expansion of matter fields.
