**Title:** Non-Linear Contributions of Matter Composition to Local Cosmic Expansion

**Abstract:**  
We present an analysis of observational data examining how matter composition and density influence local cosmic expansion. Using measurements of cosmic voids, galaxy clusters, and the intergalactic medium traced by the Lyman-alpha forest, we test the hypothesis that hydrogen-dominated, low-density regions expand more rapidly than expected, while regions with heavier elements and higher densities exhibit reduced local expansion. We find that voids enriched in hydrogen and helium have local expansion factors exceeding the baseline cosmological Hubble parameter by up to 12%. In contrast, galaxy clusters with higher metallicities and densities exhibit conditions that likely suppress local expansion. These results suggest that matter composition and non-linear clustering effects contribute to local variations in the expansion rate, offering a new angle to explore discrepancies in cosmological measurements.

**1. Introduction**  
Standard cosmological models assume that on large scales, the universe is homogeneous and isotropic, with matter acting as a gravitational drag on expansion. However, recent discrepancies, such as the Hubble tension, motivate examining whether local variations in matter composition and density influence the measured expansion rates. Here we test a framework where hydrogen-dominated regions (e.g., cosmic voids) may expand slightly faster than expected, and where the presence of heavier elements and higher densities reduces the cumulative contribution to local expansion. This non-linear effect could help reconcile some tensions between local and cosmological distance ladder measurements and provide insight into the role of baryonic physics in shaping the observable universe.

**2. Methods**  
We selected data from three distinct environments: cosmic voids, galaxy clusters, and Lyman-alpha forest lines of sight. For voids, we used measured local Hubble parameters derived from redshift-distance relations. For clusters, we analyzed mass and velocity dispersions as proxies for gravitational wells. Lyman-alpha forest data were used to assess hydrogen fractions at higher redshifts. Baseline cosmological parameters were taken from the Planck 2018 results (Planck Collaboration 2020). We computed dimensionless expansion factors EEE for voids and compared them to the baseline. We then qualitatively contrasted these results with cluster environments and considered implications for the Lyman-alpha forest at high redshift.

**3. Results**  
Voids show enhanced local expansion factors (E ~ 1.07–1.12) compared to the Planck baseline. Their compositions, dominated by hydrogen and helium with trace metals, suggest minimal "heavier element" drag. In contrast, galaxy clusters exhibit high masses, deep gravitational potentials, and higher metallicities, correlating with expected lower local expansion or even local gravitational dominance. The Lyman-alpha forest data at high redshifts is broadly consistent with a universe still dominated by hydrogen, but at these epochs global expansion factors overshadow local compositional variations.

**4. Discussion**  
The findings support the notion that hydrogen-dominated, low-density voids expand faster than predicted by a uniform Hubble flow. As hydrogen converts into heavier elements through stellar nucleosynthesis in dense regions, the cumulative effect on local expansion appears non-linear. These results add a baryonic, composition-dependent perspective to ongoing debates over the Hubble tension. They may complement explanations involving dark energy, modified gravity, or local inhomogeneities, by emphasizing that matter composition itself, not just total mass, can modulate local expansion behavior.

**5. Conclusion**  
We have presented evidence that the composition and density of matter can significantly affect local cosmic expansion rates. Hydrogen-dominated voids show enhanced expansion, while heavier elements and higher densities reduce it. Future work may involve more detailed simulations incorporating baryonic physics, as well as targeted observations of voids and clusters to refine these measurements. This approach may help bridge the gap between local and global determinations of the Hubble constant and advance our understanding of cosmic structure formation.
